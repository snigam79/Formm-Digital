01 · The VC Question
Do the unit economics work?
The VC concern is real and deserves a serious answer. Here is the full analysis — not optimistic projections, but stress-tested numbers across three scenarios. The honest answer is: it depends entirely on which acquisition channels you use.
The concern
Consumer fitness CAC is brutally high. Most apps pay ₹8,000–20,000 per paying subscriber on paid channels. At ₹499/month that is a 16–40 month payback.
Legitimate concern
The nuance
CAC is channel-specific. Gym partnerships, organic content, and B2B-driven B2C acquisition produce CAC of ₹200–800. The model works — if you choose the right channels.
Channel-dependent
The answer
At ₹499/month with 35% 12-month retention, LTV is ₹2,100. LTV:CAC ratio is 3–10× depending on channel. The economics work — with discipline on channel mix.

07 · The B2B Bridge
B2B as B2C acquisition engine
The VC's suggestion to sell to physio clinics first is not wrong — it is premature. The B2B market requires clinical validation that takes 24 months. But the B2B bridge model — selling club licences that generate consumer users as a byproduct — is available now.
Why physio clinics require waiting
Requires validation paper
Physiotherapists need peer-reviewed evidence before using a clinical tool. That paper takes 24 months minimum. Selling before it exists means selling on vibes — and physiotherapists do not buy on vibes.
Enterprise sales cycle = 6–12 months
Institutional procurement is slow. A physio clinic decision involves the clinic owner, potentially a hospital system, and risk assessment. First revenue is 18–36 months away if you start today.
No data without consumers first
The validation study requires 500+ users with 12 months of data. That data comes from the consumer product. Physio-first means no data, means no study, means no clinical sale. The consumer market is the prerequisite, not the alternative.
The B2B bridge — available now
Club licence → free B2C acquisition
Sell a cricket academy a ₹5,000/month club licence. 30 players get Formm access included. That is 30 consumer users at ₹0 marginal CAC. The B2B sale funds the B2C acquisition.
Coach portal → referral network
A coach with 10 athletes is 10 consumer users acquired through one B2B relationship. The coach becomes a distribution node. Every athlete who uses Formm at their coach's recommendation converts at 3–5× the rate of cold acquisition.
Gym chain white-label → scale
Approach gym chains for a white-labelled version. ₹10/member/month from the gym. 500 members × ₹10 = ₹5,000/month per gym. 20 gyms = ₹1L/month with near-zero consumer CAC.

08 · The Honest Verdict
Do the unit economics work?
The complete analysis. No spin. The numbers that work, the numbers that do not, and what has to be true for the business to be viable.
✓ The economics work — with the right channel mix
At blended CAC of ₹310 (50% gym partnerships, 30% organic), LTV of ₹2,100 (base case retention), and payback under 1 month, the unit economics are strong. LTV:CAC of 6.8× is well above the 3× VC benchmark. The business is viable — if you never rely primarily on paid acquisition in the early phase.
✗ The economics break on paid acquisition
At paid CAC of ₹8,000 and LTV of ₹2,100, LTV:CAC is 0.26×. Every paid-acquired user destroys ₹5,900 of value. This is the scenario the VC is worried about — and rightly so. The discipline is to never let paid acquisition become the dominant channel before retention is proven and LTV is higher.
⚠ The retention cliff is the existential risk
The entire LTV model depends on getting users to their 5th analysis — the Formm Score unlock. If users churn before then, the switching cost never forms, 12-month retention stays at 20% instead of 35%, and LTV falls to ₹1,200. At ₹1,200 LTV even gym partnership CAC (₹200) produces only 6× — acceptable but thin. The product must get users to analysis 5 within the first 30 days. That is the single most important product metric in the business.
What has to be true for the business to work
Blended CAC stays below ₹600
Achievable with gym-heavy and organic-heavy channel mix. Discipline required — no paid acquisition at scale in Year 1.
12-month retention above 30%
Requires the switching cost to form — at least 5 analyses per user within 30 days. The Formm Score unlock is the retention mechanism. Everything depends on this metric.
ARPU expansion to ₹699+ by month 18
The ₹499 plan gets the economics to marginal viability. The ₹999 professional plan (with B2B API access) gets it to strong viability. Upsell is the LTV multiplier.
B2B revenue by month 6
Club licences and coach portal must contribute revenue before the consumer product is profitable on its own. 10 club licences = ₹50K/month. This funds the team while consumer retention is proven.
Share card flywheel fires
If 15% of users share a score card and 8% of views convert to installs, the flywheel generates 12 free installs/week per 1,000 users. At 5,000 users that is 60 free installs/week — compounding. This is not guaranteed but it is the thing that makes the economics exceptional rather than merely workable.
The one-paragraph answer to the VC
"The unit economics work — with strict channel discipline. Our blended CAC of ₹310 against an LTV of ₹2,100 produces a 6.8× LTV:CAC ratio that exceeds your 3× benchmark. The consumer CAC problem you are describing is real on paid acquisition channels, which is why we will not touch paid at scale until retention is proven. Our acquisition is gym partnerships, organic content, and B2B club licences that generate consumer users as a byproduct. The single number that makes or breaks the model is 30-day retention to first score unlock. That is what we are optimising for in the product right now."
